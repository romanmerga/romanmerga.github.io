International Trade, Volatility, and Income Differences


Poor countries export less than rich countries. Furthermore, rich countries have more exporters per capita, which are larger and less likely to stop exporting. I show that these differences in trade between rich and poor countries are the consequence of poorer countries being more volatile at both the macro and the microeconomic levels. At the cross-country level, I find that 40% of the relationship between trade wedges and GDP per capita is due to volatility. Using Colombian firm-level data, I show that exporters facing higher volatility export less. Additionally, new exporters facing more volatility grow at slower rates and end up being smaller. These effects are even stronger in sectors that face higher markup elasticity. Motivated by these firm-level facts, I develop a dynamic model of international trade with endogenous mark-ups. I estimate the model to match Colombian data and the causal estimates of how mark-ups vary with firm relative size. I show that the model is able to capture the relation between exports and volatility in the data to show the relevance of these micro-level mechanisms to explain the aggregate relationships in the data. I then show that a 1% increase in micro-volatility in the dynamic model maps to a 1.15% increase in the exporter iceberg cost estimated by a standard Melitz model, explaining why estimates of iceberg cost by static models are higher in developing countries.
